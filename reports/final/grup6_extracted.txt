Abstract

Modern generative models acquire transformative capabilities when equipped with the ability to
act autonomously—planning multi-step workflows, invoking external tools, analyzing feedback
from execution, and iteratively refining their approach. This survey synthesizes 60+ papers
published between January 2023 and December 2025, systematically surveying the evolving
reinforcement learning methodologies used to build such autonomous agents. Our taxonomy
organizes findings into six domains (core policy optimization, tool orchestration, memory
architectures, multi-agent coordination, evaluation benchmarks, and safety) comprising 19
sub-categories for production deployment.
    Key Findings. We identify three primary branches of algorithms in current research. One
approach builds on Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)-style alignment, which integrates
trained reward networks with value function baselines. This method is effective but requires
significant memory. Another branch, characterized by Direct Preference Optimization (DPO),
skips explicit reward modeling by optimizing using preference rankings directly, making it
more computationally efficient for offline data. A third, increasingly popular family includes
Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), which uses group-level comparisons to optimize
the policy without needing a critic network. In our catalog of 80 variants (based on a manual
review of 60+ papers), GRPO-style methods account for 39%, highlighting their growing role
in memory-efficient training. We also examine the trade-offs between these algorithms across
various deployment contexts.
    Analytical Insights. We complement the empirical survey with theoretical discussion. We
examine why PPO-style clipping improves optimization stability in practice and how success
rates on agentic benchmarks may scale with model capacity. We also propose a simplified model
clarifying when retrieval improves factuality versus when it fails, and provide a communication-
cost analysis for common multi-agent coordination topologies.
    Empirical Results. Benchmark performance has improved substantially. SWE-bench
Verified increased from 1.7% to 49% [? ], WebArena-Lite from 6.1% to 44.8% [? ], OSWorld
from 18.4% to 73.3% [? ], and AIME 2024 from 9.3% (GPT-4 prompting baseline [? ]) to
79.8% [? ].1 These figures represent 2–29× improvements over prompting-only baselines within
24 months. (Throughout this report, “improvement” refers to relative gain over prompting-only

    Open Problems. Several fundamental challenges persist: credit assignment in long-horizon
trajectories, robust tool invocation with graceful failure handling, benchmark validity for real-
world deployment prediction, and safety considerations for agents with environmental access.
We propose a three-stage training pipeline (Supervised Fine-Tuning [SFT]→RL→Retrieval-
Augmented Generation [RAG]) with testable hypotheses designed for academic computing
resources.

Keywords: Autonomous Agents, RL-based Alignment, PPO, DPO, GRPO, RAG, Multi-Agent
Coordination, Tool Orchestration, Code Generation.

Highlights:

    • Comprehensive synthesis of 60+ papers on RL-trained agents published 2023–2025

    • Systematic comparison of PPO, DPO, and GRPO algorithm families with theoretical
      foundations

    • Analysis of GRPO adoption trends (39% of recent methods) and memory efficiency
      implications

    • Documentation of 2–29× benchmark improvements with contributing factors identified

    • Reproducible experimental framework designed for academic computing infrastructure

1.1     Why Supervised Learning Isn’t Enough
Show a model a thousand correct solutions, and it learns to generate text that mimics those
solutions. This represents pattern matching, not problem-solving. When unexpected errors occur,
surface-level similarity to training data fails; the model needs to figure out what actually works
through trial and error.
    Chess makes the distinction clear. You can memorize grandmaster games and still lose to
anyone who can calculate three moves ahead. Opening theory is useful; it’s not the whole game.
Same with agents: watching demos teaches the format, but navigating back from a failed state
requires a different kind of knowledge—the kind you only get from trial and error.
    Here’s another angle: sequential reasoning trades time for depth. A single forward pass
gives you one shot at the answer. Chain-of-thought prompting gives you multiple passes where
each step can build on the last, catch mistakes, and adjust. That’s not just “more compute”—it’s
structured compute where the model’s own outputs become working memory. GRPO (Group
Relative Policy Optimization—a memory-efficient RL algorithm we detail in Section 2.6) takes
a similar idea to training: instead of learning from one attempt per prompt, you learn from the
relative quality of multiple attempts. More signal, same data budget.

doing something and actually doing it. LLMs read mountains of text where people describe how
to fix bugs, configure servers, or navigate codebases. But that text almost never shows the messy
reality: the wrong turns, the retries, the moment when the stack trace doesn’t match anything in
Stack Overflow.

1.2.1    The Math: Agents as POMDPs
We model agentic AI as a Partially Observable MDP (POMDP) because it captures the key
constraint: agents never see everything. They get text snippets, screenshots, error messages—not
the full state of the filesystem or the browser’s internal DOM. Using a standard MDP would
pretend observability exists when it doesn’t. The POMDP tuple M = ⟨S , A , Ω, T, O, R, γ⟩
breaks down as:

    • S : The true state of the world (file contents, browser state, server responses). Hidden
      from the agent.

    • A : What the agent can do—token sequences that might be tool calls, code snippets, or
      plain text. Unlike Atari where actions are discrete button presses, here the action space is
      combinatorially huge.

    • Ω: What the agent actually sees (text, images, whatever the interface shows it).

    • T (s′ |s, a): How the environment responds to actions. Modeling this explicitly reminds us
      that environments are dynamic and unpredictable.

                                                 3
Chapter 1. Introduction


    • O(o|s): The lossy mapping from true state to observations. Critical—observations hide
      information.

    • r(s, a): The reward signal. Could be task success, human ratings, or intermediate process
      scores.

    • γ ∈ [0, 1]: How much future rewards are discounted. Tuning this trades off myopia against
      long-horizon planning.

2.1      Mechanisms of RLHF
The primary challenge is that gradient descent needs something to optimize, but “make this
response more helpful” isn’t a loss function you can differentiate. You can’t write down an
equation for what makes one answer better than another—ask ten people and you’ll get ten
different rankings. ? ] figured out a workaround: don’t ask humans to score responses on some
scale (they’re terrible at it), just ask them to pick which response is better. Collect enough of
these A-vs-B comparisons and you can train a neural network to approximate the pattern in those
choices. It’s a noisy proxy for what people actually want, but it’s enough to give gradient descent
something concrete to chase.

2.2     Limitations of RLHF and Mitigation Strategies
The vanilla RLHF setup has some ugly failure modes that won’t go away with better hyperpa-
rameters:
    Humans are slow. Every preference judgment needs a person’s eyeballs, which means your
annotation budget puts a hard ceiling on training scale. Want 10× more data? Hire 10× more
annotators.
    Reward models can be gamed. Push the optimization hard enough and your model will
discover responses that score high but read like nonsense—the reward network learned to
recognize surface patterns, not actual quality.
    Errors compound. You’re stacking three imperfect steps: SFT introduces biases, reward
modeling adds noise, and RL amplifies whatever mistakes survived. Small issues become big
ones.

Chapter 2. Foundations and Alignment Algorithms


     Researchers have tackled each of these:
     Let the model supervise itself: ? ] taught models a set of principles, then had them critique
and revise their own outputs in a loop. Harmful responses dropped substantially across their test
suite, and the human annotators barely had to lift a finger.
     Let AI do the ranking: ? ] showed you can swap human annotators for a capable LLM and
still get good alignment—at least for summarization. Annotation costs fell by more than 10×

     Borrow from behavioral economics: ? ] noticed that people feel losses more sharply than
equivalent gains (classic prospect theory stuff). Their KTO loss function bakes this asymmetry
in directly, and they got comparable results using 2–4× less training data.

2.3.1    Core Agentic Capabilities
Each capability represents a different optimization target:

    • Hierarchical Goal Decomposition: Training agents to break complex problems into
      manageable sub-problems, whether through lookahead planning or recursive planning
      with reflection.

    • Tool Mastery: Developing competence in using APIs—selecting the right ones, construct-
      ing valid calls, and interpreting results—through trajectory-level feedback.

    • Memory Management: Breaking free from rigid context limits through learned memory
      operations—deciding what to stash, when to fetch, and how to compress history without
      losing the important bits.

    • Deliberate Reasoning: Reinforcing explicit multi-step thinking (chains, trees, graphs)
      with process rewards that value valid intermediate deduction steps, not just final answers.

    • Self-Improvement: Enabling agents to learn new skills without external curriculum—
      through self-play, curiosity signals, or synthetic task generation.

    • Active Perception: For vision-equipped agents, learning where to look, when to capture
      screenshots, and how to balance observation cost against information gain.

2.3.2    Task Domains

2.3.3    Comprehensive RL Taxonomy
We organize papers into 19 categories not by convenience but by what they optimize. The
four-cluster organization reflects fundamental divisions in how agents learn. Core algorithms
differ by their optimization target: policy-based methods optimize action selection directly, value-

Learning paradigms differ by their data source: RLHF uses human preferences, imitation uses
demonstrations, offline RL uses logged data. These categories aren’t just labels—they reflect
different assumptions about what’s learnable and how to learn it.

state values (Value-Based) or directly optimizing action probabilities (Policy-Based). Figure 2.4
sketches the two main families.

3. Multi-Agent RL

No single entity can handle everything. Multi-agent systems tackle complex problems by

mirroring how human organizations trade coordination costs for total capability.

atomic actions to achieve them, reducing the search space for long tasks. Figure 2.5 sketches this

5. Safe & Constrained RL

Standard RL assumes maximizing reward is the only goal, but in reality, the path matters. Safe
RL introduces constrained optimization—agents must maximize utility while staying within
safety boundaries. This stops the agent from gaming the objective via risky or unacceptable
shortcuts.

world models even absent explicit tasks. Figure 2.6 shows how intrinsic rewards patch the

8. Imitation Learning

Searching from scratch is inefficient when solutions already exist. Imitation learning treats expert
behavior as a strong prior, narrowing the search to "proven effective strategies." This bootstraps
learning, avoiding rediscovery of basic competencies.

9. Agentic RL

This applies control theory to language and digital tools. Unlike physical control, the "action
space" involves generating tokens or API calls, and the "state space" is the context window.
The twist: reasoning is no longer static output—it is a latent policy that pokes at the digital
environment until the answer clicks into place.

10. Offline RL

Online interaction is often impossible due to safety or cost (medical treatment, power grids).
Offline RL tackles the counterfactual problem: learning solid policies from frozen historical data.
The hard part is staying conservative—avoiding actions that look good but lead to unseen states.

12. Open-Ended Learning & Generalization

Fixed benchmarks lead to overfitting. Open-ended learning mimics evolution, where environment
and agent co-evolve. The system generates its own curriculum of increasingly difficult tasks,
ensuring capabilities must constantly expand. The goal isn’t solving a task but indefinitely
increasing solvable complexity.

13. Systems & Modular RL

The bottleneck in modern AI is often data throughput, not algorithms. Systems RL tackles the
engineering physics of training: moving massive experience data from environments to learners
efficiently. It treats the training loop as a high-performance distributed computing problem.

14. Multi-Objective RL

Real problems rarely have a single success metric. Agents must balance speed against accuracy,
or profit against risk. Multi-objective RL rejects scalar rewards, instead solving for the Pareto
frontier—all optimal trade-offs. This allows dynamic selection between conflicting goals at
runtime.

15. Embodied & Robotics RL

Intelligence needs grounding in physical reality. Embodied RL deals with noisy, continuous,
unforgiving physics. The hard part is the “sim-to-real” gap: learning policies in perfect simulation
that survive real-world friction, latency, and sensor noise.

2.4     Policy Gradient Methods: PPO and TRPO
Policy optimization has a cliff-edge problem: a single bad update can destroy the policy, and
because the data distribution depends on the policy, it can never recover. PPO solves this by
enforcing a "trust region"—a mathematical guardrail ensuring we never step too far into the
unknown. Trust-region methods prevent catastrophic performance degradation by limiting policy
updates. PPO [? ] makes this practical with a clipping mechanism:

2.5     DPO: Skip the Reward Model Entirely
Transition: PPO’s multi-model architecture works but creates engineering headaches. What if
we could eliminate the reward model entirely?
   Building an independent reward model is not always necessary if the objective can be solved
algebraically. The authors of the DPO framework [? ] demonstrated that the RLHF objective
can be restructured until the reward model cancels out. What’s left is a loss you can calculate
based on pairs of preferences—no intermediate scoring step:

2.6         GRPO: The Memory-Efficient Alternative That Took Over
Transition: DPO simplifies training but sacrifices exploration. GRPO takes a different path—
keeping online learning while dramatically reducing memory requirements.
     Standard RL uses a “critic” network to estimate how good a state is, which helps reduce
variance in gradient estimates. But here’s the thing: you don’t actually need to know how good
something is in absolute terms—you just need to know whether it’s better or worse than the
alternatives. The DeepSeek-R1 team [? ] ran with this idea and stripped out the critic entirely.
Their approach, GRPO, works like this: create multiple outputs for a given query, score them
all (using whatever reward signal you have—test cases, a learned model, even just rule-based
checks), then rank them within the batch. Responses that beat the batch average get reinforced;
responses below average get penalized. That’s it.
     The implications for memory usage are significant. PPO needs to keep four models in
VRAM: the policy, a reference copy, a critic, and often a reward model. GRPO eliminates the
critic entirely, requiring only the policy and a reference model for the KL penalty (2× vs 4×
model size), plus a scoring function. For a 70B parameter model, that’s a 50% memory reduction
that can make the difference between needing multiple nodes and fitting on a single machine.
     Did it work? DeepSeek-R1 hit 79.8% on AIME 2024 math competition problems [? ],
putting it in the same ballpark as human medalists. And they did it without the infrastructure
burden that made PPO-style training prohibitively expensive for most labs.


2.6.1       The GRPO Family Tree
Once GRPO proved itself, people started tweaking it for specific use cases:

    • Flow-GRPO [? ]: Handles multi-turn conversations by computing advantages over entire


                                                   19
Chapter 2. Foundations and Alignment Algorithms


        dialogue trajectories, not just single responses. Helps agents maintain coherence across
        long exchanges instead of optimizing each reply in isolation.

    • M-GRPO [? ]: Built for multi-agent setups where a “manager” delegates subtasks to
      “workers.” Each level in the hierarchy keeps its own baseline, so agents can specialize
      without fighting over a shared reward signal.

    • GRPO-TCR: Adds a penalty for malformed tool calls. Generate an invalid API invocation
      and your advantage score takes a hit—a simple way to teach syntactic correctness without
      separate supervised training.

    • FoldGRPO: Adjusts the KL penalty on the fly. If the policy is changing too fast, tighten the
      leash; if training is stable, loosen it. This adaptive scheduling helps with the catastrophic
      forgetting problem in long-horizon tasks.

Novel PPO Variants: Parallel developments have improved PPO for agentic settings:

    • DAPO (Decoupled Clip and Dynamic Sampling Policy Optimization) [? ]: Removes
      the clipping mechanism and introduces dynamic sampling to address the entropy collapse
      problem in large-scale RL training. Achieves 50 points on AIME 2024 with Qwen2.5-32B,
      providing a fully open-source alternative to proprietary reasoning systems.

Additional Novel Algorithms (2024–2025): Beyond GRPO and PPO variants, new algorithms
have emerged:

    • Tree-GRPO [? ]: Applies tree search to LLM agent reinforcement learning, outperforming
      chain-based approaches over a selection of 11 datasets and 3 QA task types. Multi-child
      branching enables multiple policy updates per forward pass with fine-grained credit
      assignment via reward backpropagation through the tree structure.

    • ReMax [? ]: Simplifies GRPO further by using maximum-likelihood estimation on the
      best response in each group, eliminating negative gradients entirely and improving stability.

3.1     Giving Models Hands
Fundamentally, an LLM is a next-token predictor located in a datacenter. No eyes, no hands, no
network connection. If you want it to actually do things—book a flight, run a test suite, query a
database—you need to give it interfaces: ways to convert sequences of tokens into API calls and
button clicks. Think of it like wiring up nerves between a brain and muscles.
    ReAct [? ] popularized a clean way to do this: let the model alternate between thinking
out loud (“I should search for the user’s email”) and taking action (“Search[user email]”). The
thinking part keeps the reasoning interpretable; the action part keeps it grounded in what actually
happens. On ALFWorld household tasks, this interleaved approach beat action-only baselines by
34%.

training data to keep only cases where calling a tool (calculator, calendar, search) actually helped
predict the next token better. No explicit “use the calculator here” annotations—the model just
learns that certain situations benefit from external calls.

4.1     Why RAG Matters for Agents
All knowledge in a neural network is stored in its weights, which are fixed at the end of training.
The world keeps changing; the model doesn’t. Want to update its knowledge? Retrain it—which
burns GPU weeks and calendar days.
    RAG sidesteps this by splitting the problem in two. The model handles reasoning. A separate
database handles facts. Update the database and the model’s answers update instantly—no
gradient descent required. This isn’t a hack; it’s a design principle. Reasoning evolves slowly
(training is expensive), but facts evolve constantly (database writes are cheap).
    There’s another constraint: context windows have hard limits. Llama-3 caps at 128K tokens.
GPT-4 Turbo supports 128K tokens. Claude-3.5 reaches 200K tokens. Sounds like a lot until
you’re debugging a million-line codebase or synthesizing a month’s worth of communications.
Even with expanded windows, pure parametric models hit a wall because attention scales
quadratically and extended context lengths degrade retrieval accuracy.
    RAG solves this too: instead of stuffing everything into context, you retrieve what’s relevant
when you need it. The model sees the question plus the top-K passages most likely to help—
nothing more, nothing less.

Newer papers have tailored RAG for agentic workflows, often baking in RL-based tuning:

    RAHL (Retrieval-Augmented Hierarchical Learning): Separates retrieval into two levels:
(1) coarse-grained topic retrieval selecting document clusters, then (2) fine-grained passage
retrieval within selected clusters. The hierarchical approach significantly reduces search space
while maintaining high recall on targeted passages.

in them. The manager plans. The coder implements. The critic checks. Errors get caught earlier.
Parallelism speeds things up. And when one agent screws up, others can compensate.

things out. No scripting. The agents planned their days, formed relationships, gossiped. Human
raters judged 87% of behaviors as believable. Coordination emerged without anyone designing
it.
    Self-organized teams. ? ] had agents negotiate who does what through “inception
prompting”—agents defining each other’s roles on the fly. Hit 76% task completion with
zero human intervention in task allocation.
    Plug-and-play topologies. ? ] built a framework where you can wire agents together in
different patterns—pipelines, trees, meshes—without rewriting everything. Swap the topology
and 90% of the code still works.


4.2.2    RL-Trained Multi-Agent Coordination (2025 Advances)
Recent work has moved beyond hand-designed role assignments to learning coordination through
reinforcement learning:


                                              42
Chapter 4. Beyond the Basics: RAG and Multi-Agent Systems


    MATPO (Multi-Agent Trust-Region Policy Optimization) [? ]: Extends PPO’s trust
region concept to multi-agent settings where agents must coordinate without centralized control.
By maintaining separate advantage baselines per agent while sharing a joint reward signal,
MATPO achieves 18.38% improvement over single-agent approaches on evaluations focused
on collaborative reasoning. The key insight: agents learn complementary specializations—one
focuses on information retrieval while another handles synthesis.
    M-GRPO (Multi-Agent GRPO): Adapts GRPO’s group normalization for hierarchical
coordination. A manager agent learns to decompose tasks and assign them to worker agents, each
with specialized capabilities. Advantages are normalized within each hierarchy level, preventing
dominant agents from overshadowing specialists.
    Emergent Coordination Behaviors: Training multi-agent systems with shared rewards
produces emergent specialization without explicit role assignment. Documented behaviors
include:
    • Skill differentiation: Agents spontaneously specialize in retrieval vs. reasoning

    • Communication protocols: Agents develop shorthand for common subtasks

    • Error recovery handoffs: Agents learn to recognize when to delegate failed attempts
Heuristic 4.4 (Communication Overhead by Topology). For n agents exchanging messages,
communication costs per coordination round (i.e., one synchronization cycle where all agents
send and receive messages) scale differently by topology:
   1. Fully connected (decentralized): O(n2 ) pairwise messages per round—every agent talks
      to every other agent

   2. Tree/hierarchical (k levels): O(n) messages per round (up to O(log n) routing hops)—
      messages aggregate upward, decisions flow downward

   3. Centralized hub: O(n) messages per round—all agents communicate through a single
      coordinator, introducing a bottleneck and single-point-of-failure risk
These are asymptotic communication-scaling heuristics, not end-to-end performance guarantees—
constants, network latency, and message size significantly affect real-world overhead (e.g., fully
connected with n = 3 agents may outperform hierarchical due to coordination simplicity). The
optimal architecture depends on task structure: decentralized topologies offer robustness for
long-horizon tasks; hierarchical topologies enable clear decomposition; centralized topologies
minimize latency when the coordinator is fast enough.
Remark 4.5. This heuristic is consistent with the empirical success of hierarchical multi-agent
systems like MetaGPT (achieving 85.9% on HumanEval [? ]): tree-structured communication
lets these systems tackle complex software dev tasks without the O(n2 ) overhead of fully
connected coordination. However, the heuristic does not explain performance—many other
factors (agent capability, task decomposition quality, prompt design) also matter.

4.3      What Actually Goes Inside an Agent?
Setting aside speculation, we ask: what architectural patterns actually work?
    Three design philosophies. ? ] sorted architectures into buckets: symbolic (explicit rules),
neural (end-to-end learning), and hybrid (rules + learning). Hybrids win by 15–30% on long-
horizon tasks. The neural part handles messy perception; the symbolic part keeps planning from
going off the rails.
    Planning strategies compared. ? ] ran a bake-off: hierarchical decomposition (68%
success), external planner (72%), iterative self-refinement (76%). The pattern that kept winning:
feed execution results back into planning. Doesn’t matter what the agent is doing—closing the
loop helps.
    What everyone actually builds. ? ] surveyed 100+ agent implementations. Every single one
had an action module. 92% had a planning module. Only 58% had perception, and just 43% had
memory. Agents without memory struggle on anything requiring context across steps—which is
most interesting tasks.


4.3.1     Limitations of RAG and Multi-Agent Research
The RAG literature focuses predominantly on text retrieval; multimodal retrieval (images, code,
structured data) remains underexplored. Retrieval quality metrics (e.g., recall@k) do not always
correlate with downstream task performance, making evaluation methodology an open question.
For multi-agent systems, coordination overhead and failure cascades are often underreported—
papers typically highlight success cases rather than systematic failure analysis. Additionally,
most multi-agent benchmarks test pre-defined role assignments; emergent role discovery and
dynamic team composition remain largely unstudied. Communication efficiency claims should
be interpreted cautiously, as real-world latency and bandwidth constraints differ substantially
from simulated environments.

5.1     Experimental Design Philosophy
Requirement Questioning: Why run experiments at all? Without controlled comparison,
we cannot distinguish algorithmic improvements from noise. Why three benchmarks? One
is insufficient, but three orthogonal benchmarks (SWE-bench for code, WebArena for web,
OSWorld for OS) expose different failure modes efficiently. Why 8B models? Larger models
are prohibitively expensive for academic research, and 8B is the smallest size where meaningful
agentic capabilities emerge.
    Simplified Design: Three core experiments (PPO baseline, GRPO test, SFT control) on
WebArena with Llama-3-8B tackled the key question: Does GRPO demonstrate higher sample
efficiency than PPO for agentic tasks?
    This framework runs on academic compute budgets and produces interpretable results. Our
experimental framework aimed to answer questions, not just collect numbers. We prioritized three
lines of investigation: (1) Established baselines not to claim state-of-the-art, but to understand
what 8B models can actually do without specialized training; (2) Compared algorithms not to
crown a winner, but to understand which algorithmic biases matter for which task types; (3)
Evaluated RAG and tool-use not as add-ons, but as solutions to specific problems—RAG for

5.1.1    Research Hypotheses
From the papers we surveyed, we distill these testable hypotheses. We define sample efficiency
operationally as I80 : the number of environment interactions required to reach 80% of final per-
formance measured at a predetermined iteration count Nmax (we use Nmax = 10,000 interactions,
following common practice). Hypotheses avoid hard numeric thresholds where the literature
does not support them:

H1 (Sample-Efficiency Endpoint, GRPO vs. PPO): On interactive agentic benchmarks (We-
     bArena, optionally OSWorld), GRPO reaches I80 (80% of final performance at Nmax ) using
     fewer environment interactions than PPO under matched compute and reward design.
     Primary endpoint: I80 (GRPO)/I80 (PPO) < 1.

H2 (Tool-Use Training Generalization): Models fine-tuned on explicit tool-use trajectories
     (e.g., ReAct/Toolformer-style data) achieve higher WebArena task success than few-
     shot baselines, and the improvement persists on held-out websites (generalization split).
     Primary endpoints: mean TSR improvement and held-out TSR improvement.

H3 (RAG Conditional Benefit): On tasks requiring external knowledge (e.g., SWE-bench tasks
     requiring documentation lookup), RAG improves evidence-supported correctness when
     retrieval quality is high. We predict gains in factual/evidence-grounded metrics conditional
     on retrieval accuracy/coverage, and we explicitly test failure cases when retrieval is
     wrong.

H4 (Algorithm × Task Interaction): The relative advantage of PPO/DPO/GRPO depends on
     task type; we expect a statistically significant interaction effect (two-way ANOVA) between
     alignment algorithm and benchmark (SWE-bench vs. WebArena vs. OSWorld).

H5 (Scaling Scope, Aligned to Resources): If multiple model sizes are evaluated (e.g., ∼7B
     vs. ∼14B), RL fine-tuning gains will show diminishing returns with size for the task
     distributions considered. If only ∼8B models are feasible given compute constraints, H5
     is treated as a motivation/limitation rather than a tested hypothesis.

5.2.7    Reproducibility Protocol
Reproducibility is not about listing procedures—it’s about ensuring results aren’t artifacts. Fixed
seeds (42, 123, 456) across three independent runs test whether results depend on initialization
or are robust to randomness. Hyperparameters aren’t arbitrary: learning rate 2 × 10−5 is small
enough for stable updates but large enough for reasonable convergence speed. Batch size
8 reflects GPU memory constraints—smaller batches introduce more noise, larger require
distributed training. The warmup ratio 0.03 prevents early instabilities. RL-specific parameters
aren’t magic numbers: PPO clip ratio 0.2 balances exploration and exploitation based on
empirical stability studies. DPO β = 0.1 controls the KL penalty strength based on preference
noise estimates. GRPO group size 8 provides enough samples for reliable advantage estimation.
Every parameter reflects a trade-off between statistical efficiency, computational cost, and
training stability.

5.6     Key Findings and Analysis
Finding 1 — Suggestive Model-Family/Scale Discontinuity. Llama-3.2-3B failed to learn
GSM8K (training reward <3% throughout), while Qwen3.5-4B succeeded strongly (last-10:
84.7% ± 12.0%, 4 seeds). The 3B failure traces to 56% zero-loss steps from all-incorrect
groups (no gradient signal), while the 4B’s zero-loss stems from all-correct groups (productive
saturation). A G=32 control on the 3B model reduces zero-loss from 56% to 18% but yields only
5.0% accuracy—still near random—suggesting the failure is primarily capacity, not exploration.
Important caveats: the 3B (Llama) and 4B (Qwen) models differ in architecture family and
generation, not just parameter count; the 1.5B Qwen succeeds on tool calling while 3B Llama
fails on math, indicating the threshold is task-dependent. We interpret this as a suggestive
discontinuity rather than a confirmed universal capacity threshold.
    Finding 2 — Two-Phase Learning (Domain-Dependent Observation). On tool-calling
tasks, GRPO exhibits two phases: format compliance (steps 1–20, 0%→14%) followed by rapid
reasoning improvement (steps 21–25, 14%→58%). On GSM8K, Qwen3-8B shows a similar
pattern with training reward jumping from ∼18% to 78% within five steps, though the phases
are less separable. We describe this as a domain-dependent observation consistent with known
curriculum effects, rather than a novel GRPO property or confirmed “aha moment.”
    Finding 3 — SFT+GRPO Complementarity (Pilot Observation). Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct
starts GSM8K training at 79% reward (vs. 7% for Qwen3-8B base), compressing time-to-mastery.
In tool calling, SFT alone never generates tool calls (0% JSON validity), while SFT+GRPO
raises validity to 92%. In our limited comparisons (N=1 per configuration), SFT-initialized
GRPO produced the strongest results. Caveat: This comparison lacks matched-compute and
reverse-order controls, and is consistent with known warm-starting effects (ReST, STaR). We
report this as a pilot observation, not a confirmed complementarity.
    Finding 4 — MoE Routing Volatility (Single-Run Observation). Qwen3-30B MoE (3B
active parameters) reached 99% peak GSM8K training reward but exhibited 2.43× higher step-
to-step volatility than the dense 8B model (Levene’s test p = 7.0 × 10−6 ). We hypothesize
optimization interference between GRPO’s policy gradient and the router’s auxiliary load-
balancing loss. Caveat: This is a single MoE run; we did not log routing entropy or expert load

imbalance. The evidence is variance-level rather than mechanism-level and requires replication
before drawing architecture-level conclusions.
    Finding 5 — LoRA Rank Scales Initial Learning. All experiments modified only ∼0.1–
0.8% of total parameters via LoRA (rank 8–64). Our ablation shows higher rank correlates with
faster initial learning (rank 64: 47.5% first-5 avg vs. rank 8: 27.5%) and higher peak training
reward (87.5% vs. 62.5%), but all ranks converge to similar long-run averages (∼20–25%).
Important: 100% training reward (achieved on some runs) reflects per-step stochastic sampling,
not held-out performance. Our held-out GSM8K evaluation yields 83.3% ± 2.2% (5 seeds),
only +1.3pp above the base model’s 82.0% (p=0.26, not significant). The held-out accuracy is
overwhelmingly attributable to base model capability.
    Finding 6 — Synthetic vs. Real Data Gap. On tool calling, synthetic 5-tool tasks saturate
to reward >0.9 within 5 GRPO steps. Salesforce xlam-60k with diverse real-world schemas
yields 0.06–0.36 after 100 steps—a 3–8× difficulty gap. Similarly, GSM8K training reward
reaches high levels while MATH competition problems plateau at ∼14% at the same budget.
These are data distribution effects (curated vs. real/hard problems), suggesting that standard
synthetic benchmarks substantially overestimate capability.

this result uses a randomly sampled subset (not the full 164-problem harness) and is not
statistically significant (Fisher’s exact p=0.53; bootstrap 95% CI [27%, 53%] post-GRPO). We
treat HumanEval alongside GSM8K as non-agentic transfer controls that remain null—GRPO’s
positive effect is demonstrated only on the tool-calling task.

Chapter 6

Limitations and Future Directions

This chapter provides critical reflection on both the field and our survey. Section 6.1 identifies
five structural limitations in current agentic AI research (L1–L5). Section 6.2 acknowledges our
survey’s own constraints. We conclude with concrete future research directions addressing these
gaps.



6.1     Limitations of the Field
Digging into the literature, we uncovered a handful of deep-seated limitations baked into how
agentic AI research is carried out today. These aren’t mere implementation quirks—they point to
deeper structural problems in how we evaluate, replicate work, and guard against harm:
    L1: Benchmark-Reality Gap. Current benchmarks test simplified versions of real prob-
lems. They provide clear goals and measurable success criteria—but real deployment involves
ambiguous requirements, adversarial inputs, and cascading failures.
    Example: An agent scoring 49% on SWE-bench may have memorized common bug patterns
rather than learning general debugging principles. The core issue: we have no validated correla-
tion between benchmark scores and real-world utility. We may be overfitting to evaluation suites
rather than making genuine progress.
    L2: Reproducibility Constraints. State-of-the-art results (DeepSeek-R1’s 79.8% on AIME
2024, Claude-3.5’s 49% on SWE-bench) are achieved with proprietary training data, undisclosed
hyperparameters, and compute budgets exceeding academic accessibility. Our experimental
framework deliberately targets reproducibility with open models (Llama-3-8B, Qwen2.5-7B)
and documented configurations, accepting lower absolute performance for methodological
transparency.
    L3: Safety-Capability Tradeoff. Every capability improvement expands the potential for
harm [? ]. Agents capable of debugging code can introduce vulnerabilities; agents capable of
web navigation can exfiltrate data. Today’s safety guardrails (Constitutional AI, RLHF-based
refusal) offer only partial cover. Formally verifying agent behavior at scale is still an unsolved


                                               64
Chapter 6. Limitations and Future Directions


puzzle.
    L4: Metric Inadequacy. Task completion rates—the dominant metric—obscure reasoning
quality. An agent may succeed through luck or fail despite correct reasoning for 90% of the
trajectory. Step-wise evaluation methods exist [? ], though few teams have adopted them in
practice.
    L5: Computational Barriers. PPO requires 4× base model memory; even GRPO consumes
substantial GPU-hours for convergence. Those bills slow down iteration cycles and lock out
researchers who lack big-iron clusters.


6.2     Limitations of This Survey
A few caveats apply to our analysis:

    • Temporal Scope: The core survey analyzes papers from 2023–2025. We include founda-
      tional references from earlier years (e.g., PPO [? ], Constitutional AI [? ]) for context, but
      systematic coverage focuses on the target period.

    • Language Bias: We mostly stuck to English-language publications, so relevant work from
      non-English research groups may have slipped under the radar.

    • Publication Bias: Our analysis tilts toward wins; dead ends and negative findings are
      scarce in the published record, so they’re scarce here too.

    • Verification Limitations: We quote numbers straight from the papers, no independent
      replication. Some of those figures may not hold up in practice.

    • Rapid Obsolescence: The field evolves rapidly; some findings may be superseded by the
      time of publication.

6.2.2    Observed Emergent Behaviors from RL Training
One of the more eye-opening discoveries from recent RL-trained agents is the pop-up of clever
behaviors nobody coded in. This matters because it reveals what RL optimization actually
teaches: not just specific actions, but general problem-solving strategies. When test-based
rewards drive systematic debugging or compute-penalties drive cost-aware tool selection, the

                                                   66
Chapter 6. Limitations and Future Directions


takeaway is that reward signals can mold meta-cognitive strategies. These emergent behaviors
provide insights into what’s learnable:
    Code Self-Correction in Testing Loops: Agents trained on SWE-bench develop itera-
tive debugging strategies: writing initial fixes, running tests, analyzing failures, and refining
solutions—without explicit training on this loop. The behavior emerges from outcome supervi-
sion alone, a sign that test-based rewards nudge agents toward methodical debugging.
    Strategic Tool Selection: Rather than always using the most powerful tool, RL-trained
agents learn context-dependent selection. For simple calculations, they use basic operations;
for complex queries, they invoke specialized APIs. This cost-aware behavior emerges from
compute-penalized rewards without explicit efficiency training.
    Reasoning Pattern Regularities: DeepSeek-R1 and similar GRPO-trained models develop
consistent reasoning templates: problem decomposition, intermediate verification, and confidence
calibration. These patterns pop up across problem types, hinting that RL can uncover broadly
useful meta-cognitive tricks.
    Error Anticipation: Agents exposed to adversarial tool environments (via AT-GRPO) learn
proactive error handling—checking preconditions before actions, validating outputs before
proceeding, and maintaining fallback plans. This defensive programming style emerges from
training on environments with realistic failure modes.
    Communication Protocol Development (Multi-Agent): In multi-agent settings without
predefined communication channels, agents develop shorthand protocols for common coordina-
tion patterns. These emergent “languages” are task-specific but remarkably consistent across
training runs, hinting that the push to coordinate keeps landing agents on the same answer.
    What does this mean in practice? Carefully crafted rewards can coax out nuanced skills even
when we never hard-coded them. The flip side is that unprogrammed behaviors are trickier to
foresee and harder to inspect—an alignment headache we cannot ignore.


6.2.3    Research Gaps Analysis
Sifting through the literature, we spot glaring gaps between what today’s best systems can do
and what production-grade agents actually need:
    Gap 1: Benchmark-Reality Divide. Current benchmarks (SWE-bench, WebArena) rep-
resent sanitized slices of real-world complexity. Agents achieving 35% on these benchmarks
face qualitatively different challenges in production: ambiguous requirements, adversarial users,
and cascading failures. Closing this gap requires benchmarks with realistic noise, incomplete
specifications, and multi-stakeholder constraints.
    Gap 2: Compositional Generalization. Agents trained on task distributions struggle with
novel compositions of familiar primitives. An agent that can “search” and “click” may fail
at “search then click on the third result.” Such fragility hints that today’s RL losses chase
surface-level patterns instead of building transferable primitives.

Chapter 7

Conclusion

Summary of Findings
This systematic review of 60+ papers on reinforcement learning for autonomous AI agents
documents a rapidly advancing field. The 2024–2025 period saw researchers pivot sharply from
prompt-only techniques toward RL-driven fine-tuning, with benchmark performance improving
4–29× across major evaluation suites (SWE-bench: 1.7%→49%, WebArena: 6.1%→44.8%,
OSWorld: 18.4%→73.3%).


Key Takeaways
1. The GRPO Paradigm Shift. GRPO and its spin-offs now dominate, accounting for 39%
of published work. By eliminating the value/critic network (using group normalization) and
leveraging verifiable rewards without a learned reward model, GRPO halves memory require-
ments (policy + reference model only, vs. four networks for PPO). This efficiency enabled
DeepSeek-R1 to achieve 79.8% on AIME 2024—putting capable agent training within reach
of smaller labs. The proliferation of specialized variants (Flow-GRPO for multi-turn dialogue,
M-GRPO for hierarchical coordination, AT-GRPO for adversarial robustness) suggests this
lightweight approach will define near-term development.
2. Tool Mastery Is Now Baseline. Agents without retrieval and tool-calling abilities encounter
fundamental capability limitations. The emergence of RL-optimized tool learning frameworks—
ReTool achieving 67% on AIME 2024, DeepAgent executing 100+ step trajectories—demonstrates
that tool integration is now essential for production deployment.
3. Multi-Agent Coordination Matures. When single agents fail, coordinated multi-agent
systems often succeed. MATPO’s 18.38% improvement over single-agent baselines and Mobile-
Agent-v3’s 73.3% on OSWorld show that RL-trained multi-agent coordination is now production-
ready. Caveat: These gains come with coordination overhead—increased latency, O(n2 ) com-
munication costs for fully-connected topologies, and failure propagation risks—that deployment


                                             70
Chapter 7. Conclusion


teams must factor into system design.
4. Emergent Capabilities and Risks. RL training produces behaviors not explicitly pro-
grammed: code self-correction, strategic tool selection, communication protocol development,
and cross-validation strategies. Put differently, a well-shaped reward can unlock nuanced skills—
yet these unplanned behaviors are difficult to anticipate or audit, presenting significant challenges
for alignment research.


Implications for Research and Practice
For researchers, we flag seven gaps that matter because they block real-world deployment—
they are not mere academic footnotes. The benchmark-reality divide means improvements
on sanitized benchmarks don’t translate to messy reality—this undermines the entire evalua-
tion paradigm. Long-horizon planning fails beyond 20 steps because credit assignment breaks
down—this is algorithmic, not a data problem. Safety verification remains informal because
formal methods face state-space explosion—this is computational complexity, not implemen-
tation. Multi-modal grounding fails because vision and language representations don’t align
naturally—this is architectural. Sample efficiency remains low because every environment
interaction is expensive—this is economic. Interpretability fails because emergent behaviors
are unpredictable—this is foundational. Each gap reflects a structural constraint, not a missing
feature.
    For practitioners, we recommend a three-stage training pipeline (SFT→RL→RAG) with
GRPO as the default algorithm for resource-constrained settings. Our ablation grid enables
systematic evaluation of design choices within typical academic compute budgets.
    For policymakers, the rapid capability gains documented here—with minimal additional
training overhead—make it clear that governance frameworks need to land before mass rollout.
The emergent-behavior findings, in particular, deserve a close look.


Broader Impact
Autonomous AI agents mark a tectonic shift—from passive tools to goal-chasing systems. Tools
respond to commands; agents pursue objectives over extended time horizons without step-by-
step instruction. This is not just incremental improvement—it’s a different paradigm. The
implications are profound because the failure modes are different: tools fail by not responding;
agents fail by pursuing the wrong goal, by getting stuck in local optima, by taking unsafe
shortcuts. The risks scale with capability because the action space grows exponentially with
horizon length. This creates a safety-capability frontier: capability improvements without safety
measures are dangerous; safety measures that cripple capability are useless. The tradeoff is
structural, not optional. This means:



                                                71
Chapter 7. Conclusion


    • Positive: Democratized automation of complex knowledge work, improved accessibility,
      and augmented human capability across software engineering, research, and creative
      domains.

    • Risks: Potential for cascading failures in agentic chains, difficulty auditing emergent
      behaviors, and amplification of existing biases in autonomous decision-making.

    The current approach of capability-first, safety-later is fundamentally flawed. Once you have
a capable system, retrofitting safety is like adding seatbelts to a car after it’s built—possible, but
much harder than designing them in from the start. Worse, emergent behaviors are unpredictable.
You cannot bolt on interpretability after the fact if the system’s reasoning is opaque. Robustness
cannot be added as a layer if the core architecture is brittle. Safety verification cannot be done
post-hoc if the state space is intractable. These must be co-equal objectives from the beginning
because they constrain the design space. A system optimized purely for capability will inevitably
use shortcuts that violate safety—this is optimization pressure, not malice. The only solution is
to bake in constraints from the start.


Future Directions
Our experiments (Chapter 5) tested several hypotheses from this survey across Llama-3.2-3B,
Qwen3.5-4B, Qwen3-8B, and Qwen3-30B-MoE using GRPO with LoRA/QLoRA. Key results
include learned schema-valid tool-call emission (0%→92% JSON validity), a suggestive capacity
discontinuity between 3B–4B, and null transfer results on GSM8K (p=0.26) and HumanEval
(p=0.53). All conclusions are specific to our low-budget QLoRA/LoRA regime. Future work
should add PPO/DPO baselines, full-harness evaluations, KL/entropy logging, and reward
function ablations. We hope other researchers will replicate, extend, and critique what we have
started here.
    The goal isn’t abstract or academic. Building agents that remain both capable and safe over
long, messy interactions is the defining challenge because shorter interactions are already solved.
The fundamental issue is scaling: as interaction length grows linearly, the probability of failure
grows exponentially. Each step introduces new error modes: tool failures, misinterpretations,
compounding inaccuracies, unexpected states. A 100-step interaction isn’t 10x harder than a
10-step interaction—it’s exponentially harder because error recovery must work across much
longer horizons. "Messy" matters because clean, well-defined tasks are already tractable. The
real world is ambiguous, adversarial, and constantly changing. Solving this requires agents
that can handle uncertainty, detect their own failures, and recover autonomously. This isn’t
incremental progress; it’s crossing a threshold from narrow AI to systems that can actually
operate in the real world.
